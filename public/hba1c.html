What does your
HbA1c result mean?

Blood Sugar
⚠️ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.
What this test measures

The HbA1c (Hemoglobin A1c or glycated hemoglobin) test measures your average blood sugar level over the past 2–3 months. Unlike a fasting glucose test that shows your sugar right now, HbA1c reflects how much glucose has been sticking to your red blood cells over time — giving your doctor a much longer window into your blood sugar control.

It's the primary test used to diagnose and monitor prediabetes and type 2 diabetes.

Reference ranges
HbA1c Level What It Means Status
Below 5.7% Normal blood sugar control Normal
5.7% – 6.4% Higher than ideal — prediabetes range Prediabetes
6.5% or higher Consistent with a diabetes diagnosis Diabetes
Above 8.0% (if diabetic) Poor blood sugar control, increased risk of complications Action Needed

Reference ranges may vary slightly by lab. Always compare your result to the range printed on your own report.

Symptoms associated with abnormal levels
↑ If High (Elevated Blood Sugar)
  • Excessive thirst and urination
  • Unexplained fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Slow-healing wounds or cuts
  • Frequent infections
  • Tingling or numbness in hands/feet
  • Increased hunger despite eating
↓ If Very Low (Hypoglycemia Risk)
  • Shakiness or trembling
  • Sweating and chills
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Irritability or mood changes
  • Confusion or difficulty concentrating

Many people with prediabetes or early type 2 diabetes have no symptoms at all — which is why routine testing matters.

Common causes of a high HbA1c
  • 🍞
    Diet high in refined carbs and sugar — Frequent blood sugar spikes from processed foods, sugary drinks, and white bread gradually raise your A1c over time.
  • 🛋️
    Physical inactivity — Muscles use glucose for energy. Less movement means less glucose is cleared from the bloodstream.
  • ⚖️
    Excess body weight (especially belly fat) — Visceral fat increases insulin resistance, making it harder for your cells to absorb sugar.
  • 💊
    Certain medications — Corticosteroids (like prednisone), some antipsychotics, and diuretics can raise blood sugar levels.
  • 😓
    Chronic stress — Stress hormones like cortisol raise blood glucose, and chronic stress can push A1c higher over time.
  • 🧬
    Family history and genetics — Type 2 diabetes has a strong hereditary component. A family history significantly increases risk.
Related tests your doctor may order
Fasting Glucose Measures blood sugar after an 8-hour fast. Used alongside A1c to diagnose diabetes.
Fasting Insulin Checks how much insulin your pancreas is producing — helps identify insulin resistance early.
Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) Tests how your body processes a sugar drink over 2 hours. Used to diagnose gestational diabetes.
C-Peptide Measures insulin production by the pancreas — helps distinguish type 1 from type 2 diabetes.
Comprehensive Metabolic Panel Checks kidney function, liver enzymes, and electrolytes — often ordered with A1c to assess diabetes complications.
Lipid Panel High blood sugar is closely linked to high triglycerides and low HDL — these often appear together.
Frequently asked questions
Do I need to fast before an HbA1c test?
No. Unlike a fasting glucose test, HbA1c doesn't require fasting. You can eat and drink normally before the blood draw. This makes it a very convenient test for routine monitoring.
How quickly can I lower my HbA1c?
Since A1c reflects a 2–3 month average, meaningful changes typically show up after 3 months of sustained effort. Diet changes, regular exercise, weight loss, and medication (if prescribed) can all help lower it. Dropping 0.5–1% per 3-month period is a realistic goal for many people.
Can conditions other than diabetes affect my HbA1c?
Yes. Iron-deficiency anemia, hemolytic anemia, kidney disease, and certain genetic variants of hemoglobin (like sickle cell trait) can all give falsely high or falsely low A1c readings. If your result seems inconsistent with your symptoms, ask your doctor about alternative tests.
What's the difference between HbA1c and blood glucose?
Blood glucose (fingerstick or fasting lab test) is a snapshot of your sugar level at one moment. HbA1c is the long-term average — it tells you about patterns, not just the moment of the test. Both are useful; they give different information.
Is a 5.8% A1c something I should worry about?
A 5.8% falls in the prediabetes range (5.7–6.4%). It's not diabetes yet, but it is a signal that blood sugar control is slipping. The good news: prediabetes is reversible with lifestyle changes in many people. This is the best time to act — before it becomes full diabetes.
Questions to ask your doctor
  • What exactly does my A1c number mean for my health right now?
  • Am I in the prediabetes range, and if so, what's my risk of progressing to type 2?
  • What A1c target should I be aiming for, given my age and health history?
  • Should I also test my fasting glucose or get an oral glucose tolerance test?
  • Are there specific diet or exercise changes that would help my numbers the most?
  • How often should I retest — every 3 months or once a year?
  • Could any of my medications be affecting this result?
What to do next
If your A1c is in the prediabetes or diabetes range, don't panic — this is actionable information. Even modest lifestyle changes (cutting refined carbs, adding 30 minutes of walking most days, losing 5–7% of body weight) can meaningfully lower your A1c. If you're already diagnosed with diabetes, consistent monitoring every 3 months helps your care team adjust your treatment plan to keep your numbers in range.
