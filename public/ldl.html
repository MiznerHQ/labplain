What does your
LDL Cholesterol result mean?

Heart Health
⚠️ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.
What this test measures

LDL (low-density lipoprotein) is often called "bad" cholesterol because it carries cholesterol particles throughout your body and can deposit them in artery walls. Over time, this buildup — called plaque — narrows your arteries and raises the risk of heart attack and stroke. Your LDL number is one of the most important markers your doctor uses to assess cardiovascular risk.

Normal reference range
Optimal: Below 100 mg/dL  |  Near optimal: 100–129 mg/dL
↑ Borderline–Very High

130–159 mg/dL borderline high. 160–189 mg/dL high. 190+ mg/dL very high — often requires medication.

↓ Low (Generally Good)

Very low LDL is usually positive. Levels under 40 mg/dL may rarely signal malnutrition or hyperthyroidism.

Reference ranges vary by lab and by your personal cardiovascular risk profile. Always compare to the range on your own report.

Symptoms & Signs

High LDL has no symptoms — that's what makes it dangerous. Most people feel completely normal while plaque quietly builds in their arteries for years. Symptoms only appear if high LDL has already caused serious damage:

Chest pain (angina)Reduced blood flow to the heart from narrowed arteries.
XanthomasYellowish fat deposits under the skin, especially around eyes or tendons — sign of very high LDL.
Corneal arcusA gray-white ring around the iris — more significant in people under 45.
Leg pain when walkingPeripheral artery disease from plaque buildup in leg arteries.

Common Causes of High LDL

Lifestyle factors
  • Diet high in saturated and trans fats
  • Physical inactivity
  • Obesity or excess weight
  • Smoking
  • Heavy alcohol use
Medical conditions
  • Familial hypercholesterolemia (genetic)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Certain medications (steroids, diuretics)

Frequently Asked Questions

Is LDL the same as total cholesterol?
No. Total cholesterol includes LDL, HDL (good cholesterol), and VLDL. LDL is just one component — and usually the most important one for assessing heart disease risk.
Can I lower my LDL without medication?
Yes, for many people. A diet low in saturated fat, regular aerobic exercise, weight loss, and quitting smoking can reduce LDL by 10–30%. But if LDL is very high or you have other risk factors, medication may still be needed.
What's the difference between LDL and HDL?
LDL carries cholesterol to your arteries (bad). HDL carries cholesterol away from arteries back to the liver for removal (good). You want LDL low and HDL high.
How often should LDL be checked?
Adults over 20 should have a full lipid panel every 4–6 years if healthy. If you have risk factors or are on cholesterol medication, your doctor may check it annually or more often.
Do statins always lower LDL?
Statins are very effective and lower LDL by 30–50% on average. Some people have side effects like muscle aches. There are also non-statin options (ezetimibe, PCSK9 inhibitors) if needed.
