CRP (C-Reactive Protein) Test

Your body's inflammation alarm — what high CRP means, what causes it, and what to do next. In plain English.

What Is CRP?

C-reactive protein (CRP) is a protein your liver produces in response to inflammation anywhere in your body. Think of it as your body's general-purpose alarm signal — when something is wrong, CRP shoots up fast.

CRP doesn't tell your doctor where the inflammation is coming from, but it confirms that inflammation is happening. That makes it one of the most useful initial screening markers in medicine. It rises within hours of an inflammatory trigger and can double every 8 hours.

There are two versions of the test: Standard CRP (used to detect acute infection or illness) and High-Sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) (used to assess heart disease and chronic inflammation risk). Make sure you know which one was ordered.

⚠️ Important: A single CRP result is rarely definitive. Your doctor will interpret it alongside your symptoms, other labs, and medical history. Recent exercise, a minor cold, or even a bad night's sleep can elevate CRP temporarily.

Symptoms of High Inflammation

Elevated CRP is a lab finding — not a symptom itself. But the inflammation causing it often produces:

🔥Fever or chills
😩Fatigue or weakness
💪Joint pain or swelling
🤕Muscle aches
🫁Chest pain or tightness
🌡️Redness or warmth at a site
🤢Nausea or loss of appetite
🧠Brain fog or confusion

In some people — especially with chronic, low-grade inflammation — there are no obvious symptoms. This is why hs-CRP is particularly valuable for silent cardiovascular risk.

What Causes High CRP?

🦠 Infections

Bacterial infections cause the sharpest CRP spikes. Viral infections raise it too, but usually less dramatically.

🦴 Autoimmune Conditions

Rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, IBD, and other autoimmune diseases chronically activate the inflammatory response.

❤️ Heart Disease

Atherosclerosis involves chronic vascular inflammation. Elevated hs-CRP is an independent predictor of heart attack risk.

🩸 Tissue Injury

Surgery, trauma, burns, or even intense exercise can spike CRP as the body repairs itself.

🫀 Chronic Disease

Diabetes, obesity, kidney disease, and metabolic syndrome are all linked to persistently elevated CRP levels.

🚬 Lifestyle Factors

Smoking, poor sleep, high-stress lifestyle, and a poor diet all contribute to baseline inflammation.

What About Low CRP?

A low CRP (below 1.0 mg/L) is generally reassuring — it suggests no significant active inflammation. However:

  • Some inflammatory conditions (like lupus) occasionally show normal CRP despite active disease
  • CRP is not a cancer screening marker on its own
  • Low CRP does not rule out all serious conditions — it must be interpreted with clinical context

If your CRP is low but you still feel unwell, your doctor will look at other markers to investigate further.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between CRP and hs-CRP? +
Standard CRP measures larger elevations typical of active infection or illness (range: 0–350 mg/L). High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) detects smaller elevations in the 0–10 mg/L range, used specifically to assess cardiovascular disease risk in people who appear healthy. The test measures the same molecule — just at different sensitivity levels.
Can I lower my CRP? +
Yes. For chronically elevated CRP, lifestyle changes are highly effective: regular moderate exercise, a Mediterranean-style diet, quitting smoking, reducing alcohol, improving sleep quality, and managing stress all reduce systemic inflammation. Statins are sometimes prescribed specifically to lower hs-CRP in high-risk cardiovascular patients, even when cholesterol is normal.
How quickly does CRP change? +
CRP is one of the fastest-responding inflammatory markers. It can double every 8 hours, peaking at 24–72 hours after an inflammatory trigger. Once the trigger resolves, CRP typically returns to normal within 3–7 days. This rapid response makes it useful for monitoring treatment effectiveness.
Does high CRP mean I have cancer? +
Not necessarily. While some cancers can cause elevated CRP (especially those involving tissue destruction or immune activation), CRP elevation is far more commonly caused by infection, autoimmune conditions, or injury. CRP alone is not a cancer screening test. If your doctor suspects cancer, they'll order specific tumor markers and imaging.
Should I fast before a CRP test? +
No fasting is required for a standard CRP test. However, for hs-CRP (cardiovascular risk assessment), it's sometimes done alongside a fasting lipid panel. Follow your doctor's specific instructions. Avoiding intense exercise 24 hours before the test is a good idea, as strenuous physical activity can temporarily elevate CRP.
What if my CRP is very high — over 100 mg/L? +
CRP above 100 mg/L almost always indicates a serious bacterial infection, sepsis, or major tissue injury. This level of elevation typically prompts urgent medical attention and often requires hospitalization for investigation and treatment. If you received a result this high, contact your doctor immediately if you haven't already.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • Is this standard CRP or high-sensitivity CRP — and what was it ordered to evaluate?
  • What level of concern does my result warrant given my symptoms and history?
  • Do I need follow-up testing to find the source of inflammation?
  • Should we retest in a few weeks to see if this is a temporary spike or a trend?
  • Are there lifestyle changes I should make to reduce my chronic inflammation?
  • Does my CRP result change anything about my heart disease risk assessment?
