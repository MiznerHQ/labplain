Triglycerides Blood Test

What your results mean, what causes abnormal levels, and when to talk to your doctor.

What Are Triglycerides?

Triglycerides are the most common type of fat in your body. When you eat, your body converts any calories it doesn't need right away into triglycerides, which are stored in fat cells and released for energy between meals.

Triglycerides are measured as part of a standard lipid panel (cholesterol test). High triglycerides — called hypertriglyceridemia — are a major risk factor for heart disease, stroke, and pancreatitis.

Results are measured in milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL).

Normal Triglyceride Ranges

The American Heart Association and NIH use these standard categories for adults:

CategoryLevel (mg/dL)Status
NormalLess than 150Normal
Borderline high150 – 199Borderline
High200 – 499High
Very high500 or aboveVery High
OptimalLess than 100Optimal

Triglycerides must be measured after fasting for 9–12 hours for accurate results. Eating before the test can cause falsely elevated numbers.

Symptoms of Abnormal Triglycerides

High triglycerides usually cause no symptoms on their own — which is why regular testing matters. However, very high levels or associated conditions can cause noticeable signs.

⬆ High Triglycerides

  • Usually no symptoms (silent)
  • Xanthomas — fatty deposits under skin
  • Xanthelasmas — yellowish patches near eyes
  • Abdominal pain (if very high)
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Pancreatitis (levels above 500)
  • Enlarged liver or spleen
  • Milky-appearing blood (extreme levels)

⬇ Low Triglycerides

  • Rarely causes symptoms alone
  • May signal malnutrition
  • Associated with hyperthyroidism
  • Seen with very low-fat diets
  • Can occur with malabsorption disorders
  • May appear with certain medications

What Causes Abnormal Triglyceride Levels?

Causes of High Triglycerides

  • Diet high in refined carbs and sugar — white bread, soda, pastries, and alcohol are the most common dietary culprits
  • Obesity — excess body fat, especially around the abdomen, drives up triglyceride production
  • Type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance — impaired insulin function allows fat to accumulate in the bloodstream
  • Hypothyroidism — low thyroid hormone slows the breakdown of triglycerides
  • Kidney disease — reduced kidney function impairs lipid metabolism
  • Liver disease — the liver plays a central role in processing fats
  • Medications — beta-blockers, steroids, diuretics, antipsychotics, estrogen, and tamoxifen can all raise levels
  • Familial hypertriglyceridemia — a genetic condition causing chronically elevated levels
  • Pregnancy — triglycerides naturally rise during the third trimester

Causes of Low Triglycerides

  • Very low-fat diet — extreme dietary fat restriction reduces triglyceride production
  • Hyperthyroidism — an overactive thyroid accelerates fat breakdown
  • Malnutrition or malabsorption — conditions like celiac disease or Crohn's disease can impair fat absorption
  • Certain medications — statins, fibrates, niacin, and fish oil supplements actively lower triglycerides

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need to fast before a triglyceride test?
Yes — ideally 9 to 12 hours of fasting before the blood draw. Eating beforehand, especially carbohydrates or fat, can temporarily spike your triglycerides by 20–30% and give a false result. Water is fine to drink.
Can I lower my triglycerides without medication?
Yes, lifestyle changes are often very effective. Cutting sugar and refined carbs, reducing alcohol, losing even 5–10% of body weight, exercising regularly, and eating more omega-3 rich foods (salmon, sardines, flaxseed) can significantly lower levels — sometimes by 50% or more.
What's the difference between triglycerides and cholesterol?
Both are fats (lipids) in your blood, but they serve different roles. Triglycerides store unused calories for energy. Cholesterol is used to build cells and hormones. Both are measured on a lipid panel, and both can contribute to heart disease when elevated.
How dangerous are very high triglycerides?
Levels above 500 mg/dL significantly raise the risk of acute pancreatitis — a painful and potentially life-threatening inflammation of the pancreas. Levels above 1,000 mg/dL are a medical emergency requiring immediate treatment.
Can alcohol affect my triglyceride test?
Yes — even moderate alcohol consumption can significantly raise triglycerides, sometimes doubling them. You should avoid alcohol for at least 24 hours before a triglyceride test for an accurate result.
How quickly can triglycerides improve with diet changes?
Triglycerides respond relatively quickly to dietary changes — some people see significant improvement within 4–6 weeks of cutting sugar and alcohol. They're more diet-responsive than LDL cholesterol, so lifestyle changes can make a big difference fast.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Bring this list to your next appointment:

  • What is causing my triglycerides to be elevated?
  • Do I need to make dietary changes, and what specifically?
  • Should I be concerned about my heart disease risk?
  • Do I need medication, or can lifestyle changes be enough?
  • How often should I retest my lipid panel?
  • Could any of my current medications be raising my triglycerides?
  • Should I be tested for diabetes or insulin resistance?
  • Is my level high enough to risk pancreatitis?
