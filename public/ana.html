ANA Test (Antinuclear Antibody)

A positive ANA doesn't mean you have lupus — here's what it actually means and what comes next.

What Is the ANA Test?

The ANA (antinuclear antibody) test checks whether your immune system is producing antibodies that attack your own cell nuclei. It's a screening tool — not a diagnosis — used when doctors suspect an autoimmune condition like lupus, Sjögren's syndrome, or rheumatoid arthritis.

Key point: About 15–20% of healthy people have a low positive ANA. A positive result alone means very little without symptoms and follow-up testing.

Understanding Your Result

ANA results are reported as a titer (a ratio) and a pattern. Both matter.

The higher the titer, the more antibodies present — but higher doesn't always mean sicker.

ANA Patterns and What They Suggest

The lab also reports a staining pattern, which hints at which specific antibody might be involved.

  • Homogeneous (diffuse): Most common — associated with lupus and drug-induced lupus
  • Speckled: Associated with Sjögren's, mixed connective tissue disease, scleroderma
  • Nucleolar: Most strongly associated with scleroderma (systemic sclerosis)
  • Centromere: Associated with limited scleroderma (CREST syndrome)
  • Peripheral (rim): Rare — highly specific for lupus when present

Symptoms That Make a Positive ANA Meaningful

A positive ANA paired with these symptoms is much more significant than a positive result alone.

  • Persistent joint pain, swelling, or stiffness — especially in multiple joints
  • Butterfly-shaped rash across the cheeks and nose (malar rash)
  • Extreme fatigue that doesn't improve with rest
  • Fever without a clear cause
  • Sensitivity to sunlight causing rashes or flares
  • Dry eyes and dry mouth (suggests Sjögren's syndrome)
  • Raynaud's phenomenon — fingers/toes turn white or blue in the cold
  • Hair loss or mouth sores
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath

What Can Cause a Positive ANA?

Many things can trigger a positive result — autoimmune disease is only one of them.

  • Autoimmune diseases: Lupus (SLE), Sjögren's syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, scleroderma, polymyositis
  • Infections: Epstein-Barr virus (mono), hepatitis C, parvovirus, HIV
  • Medications: Hydralazine, procainamide, isoniazid, minocycline, TNF inhibitors
  • Thyroid disease: Hashimoto's or Graves' disease can trigger a low positive
  • Healthy individuals: Up to 20% of normal people — especially women over 65 — test positive at low titers
  • Cancer: Rarely, some cancers produce ANA-like antibodies

Follow-Up Tests Your Doctor May Order

A positive ANA is the beginning of a workup, not the end. These more specific tests help narrow down the cause.

  • Anti-dsDNA: Highly specific for lupus — also used to monitor disease activity
  • Anti-Smith (anti-Sm): Very specific for lupus, less sensitive
  • Anti-SSA/SSB (Ro/La): Associated with Sjögren's syndrome and neonatal lupus
  • Anti-Scl-70 (anti-topoisomerase I): Associated with diffuse scleroderma
  • Anti-centromere: Associated with limited scleroderma/CREST
  • Anti-Jo-1: Associated with polymyositis and dermatomyositis
  • Complement levels (C3, C4): Low levels can indicate active lupus
  • CBC, CMP, urinalysis: Check for organ involvement (kidney, liver, blood)

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a positive ANA mean I have lupus?
No. A positive ANA is one criterion for lupus — but you need several clinical and lab findings to receive a lupus diagnosis. Many people with positive ANAs never develop any autoimmune disease.
Can ANA levels change over time?
Yes. ANA titers can fluctuate — they may rise during illness or stress and fall when you're well. In people with autoimmune disease, titers don't always correlate with how active or severe the disease is.
Should I be worried about a 1:80 result?
Probably not on its own. A 1:80 is considered low positive and found in roughly 10–15% of healthy people. Your doctor will evaluate it alongside your symptoms and medical history.
Why did my doctor order this test?
ANA is typically ordered when someone has unexplained joint pain, rashes, fatigue, or other symptoms that suggest an autoimmune process. It's a broad screening test, not a definitive diagnosis.
Can medications cause a false positive?
Yes — this is called drug-induced lupus. Common culprits include hydralazine (blood pressure), procainamide (heart rhythm), isoniazid (tuberculosis), and some biologics. It typically resolves after stopping the medication.
Do I need to see a specialist?
If your ANA is 1:160 or higher and you have symptoms, a referral to a rheumatologist is usually appropriate. If your result is low positive and you feel well, your primary care doctor may simply monitor you over time.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  • What titer and pattern did my ANA come back as?
  • Do my symptoms match any autoimmune condition?
  • What follow-up tests do you recommend based on this result?
  • Should I see a rheumatologist?
  • Could any of my medications have caused this positive result?
  • How often should I retest if we're monitoring this?
  • What symptoms should prompt me to call you before my next appointment?
