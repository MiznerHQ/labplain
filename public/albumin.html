Albumin Blood Test

Albumin is the most abundant protein in your blood. Your results reveal important information about your liver, kidneys, and overall nutritional health.

Normal Range: 3.4 – 5.4 g/dL

What Do Your Results Mean?

Low
Below 3.4 g/dL
May indicate liver disease, kidney problems, malnutrition, or chronic inflammation. Requires follow-up.
Normal
3.4 – 5.4 g/dL
Your albumin level is within the healthy range. Your liver and kidneys are likely doing their job.
High
Above 5.4 g/dL
Usually caused by dehydration. Rarely serious on its own, but worth discussing with your doctor.

What Is Albumin?

Albumin is a protein made by your liver. It makes up about 60% of total protein in your blood and performs several essential jobs: it carries hormones, vitamins, and drugs through your bloodstream, helps maintain the right fluid balance between your blood vessels and surrounding tissue, and acts as a general indicator of your body's nutritional status and liver function.

Because albumin is produced by the liver and filtered by the kidneys, abnormal levels are often one of the first signs that something is off with either organ — even before symptoms appear.

Why it's tested: Albumin is routinely included in a Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP). Doctors also order it specifically when evaluating liver disease, kidney disease, malnutrition, or unexplained swelling.

Symptoms of Low Albumin

Low albumin (hypoalbuminemia) can cause noticeable symptoms, especially when levels drop significantly:

  • Swelling in legs, ankles, or feet (edema)
  • Puffy face or abdomen
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Poor wound healing
  • Muscle wasting or weakness
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fluid buildup in the belly (ascites)
  • Frequent infections
Why the swelling? Albumin helps keep fluid inside your blood vessels. When albumin is low, fluid leaks out into surrounding tissues — causing visible puffiness and swelling.

Common Causes of Low Albumin

  • Liver disease (cirrhosis, hepatitis)
  • Kidney disease / nephrotic syndrome
  • Malnutrition or poor protein intake
  • Inflammatory conditions (Crohn's, lupus)
  • Burns or major trauma
  • Celiac disease or malabsorption
  • Congestive heart failure
  • Thyroid disorders

Common Causes of High Albumin

High albumin is much less common and is rarely a sign of serious disease. The most frequent cause is:

  • Dehydration (most common)
  • High-protein diet
  • Prolonged use of a tourniquet during blood draw
  • Certain medications (steroids, insulin)

If your albumin is high, your doctor will likely ask about your fluid intake and recheck after adequate hydration.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I have low albumin without symptoms?

Yes. Mild decreases in albumin often cause no noticeable symptoms and are discovered only through routine blood work. Significant symptoms typically appear when albumin drops below 2.5 g/dL.

Does eating more protein raise my albumin?

If low albumin is caused by poor nutrition, increasing dietary protein can help — but it takes weeks to months for levels to recover. If the cause is liver or kidney disease, diet alone won't fix the underlying problem.

Is albumin the same as total protein?

No. Total protein measures all proteins in your blood (albumin plus globulins). Albumin is tested separately and is a more sensitive marker for liver function and nutritional status.

Can medications affect my albumin level?

Yes. Corticosteroids, insulin, and anabolic steroids can raise albumin. Estrogens and certain diuretics may lower it. Always tell your doctor about all medications and supplements you're taking.

How long does it take for albumin levels to change?

Albumin has a half-life of about 20 days, meaning changes in production or loss take weeks to show up in test results. This makes it a marker of longer-term nutritional status rather than acute changes.

Related Tests Your Doctor May Order

  • Total Protein
  • ALT (liver enzyme)
  • AST (liver enzyme)
  • ALP (alkaline phosphatase)
  • Bilirubin
  • Creatinine (kidney function)
  • BUN (blood urea nitrogen)
  • Prealbumin (faster indicator)
  • Urinalysis (protein in urine)
  • CRP (inflammation marker)

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  1. What's causing my albumin to be outside the normal range?
  2. Do I need additional liver or kidney tests to find the cause?
  3. Should I change my diet or protein intake?
  4. How quickly do you expect my levels to normalize?
  5. Are any of my medications affecting my albumin?
  6. When should I come back for a follow-up test?
