What does your
Fasting Glucose result mean?

Diabetes / Metabolic
⚠️ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.
What this test measures

Fasting blood glucose measures the amount of sugar (glucose) in your blood after you haven't eaten for at least 8 hours. Glucose is your body's primary fuel source — every cell depends on it for energy. After you eat, glucose enters your bloodstream and your pancreas releases insulin to help cells absorb it. When this system works well, blood sugar stays within a healthy range.

The fasting glucose test captures your baseline blood sugar level — free from the temporary spike that follows a meal. It's one of the most important screening tools for diabetes and prediabetes, and it's also used to monitor people who already have diabetes to see how well their blood sugar is being controlled.

This test is typically included in a Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) or Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP), or ordered on its own as part of a diabetes screening.

Normal reference range
Normal: 70–99 mg/dL  |  Prediabetes: 100–125 mg/dL  |  Diabetes: 126+ mg/dL

These are fasting ranges — meaning you should not have eaten for at least 8 hours before the test. A non-fasting glucose result is interpreted differently and is less useful for diagnosing diabetes.

⏰ Did you fast properly?

Even a small snack or sugary drink before the test can significantly raise your result. Water is fine. Black coffee can also slightly affect results. If you didn't fast for the full 8 hours, let your doctor know — they may want to repeat the test.

What your result might indicate
↑ If High

Elevated fasting glucose suggests your body isn't managing blood sugar efficiently — either your pancreas isn't producing enough insulin or your cells aren't responding to it properly. A single elevated result doesn't confirm diabetes; a second test is needed.

↓ If Low

Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) can result from fasting too long, diabetes medications, excessive alcohol, or certain medical conditions. Mild cases are common; severe hypoglycemia can be dangerous and requires prompt treatment.

Symptoms of abnormal blood sugar

↑ High Blood Sugar Symptoms

  • Increased thirst and dry mouth
  • Frequent urination, especially at night
  • Unexplained fatigue
  • Blurred vision
  • Slow-healing cuts or wounds
  • Frequent infections
  • Tingling or numbness in hands/feet
  • Unexplained weight loss

↓ Low Blood Sugar Symptoms

  • Shakiness or trembling
  • Sweating
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Irritability or anxiety
  • Hunger or nausea
  • Confusion or difficulty concentrating
  • Pale skin
Understanding prediabetes

Prediabetes is reversible

A fasting glucose between 100–125 mg/dL means you have prediabetes — blood sugar that's higher than normal but not yet high enough to be classified as type 2 diabetes. This is a critical window. Research consistently shows that lifestyle interventions at this stage can prevent or significantly delay the progression to type 2 diabetes.

The most effective changes include losing 5–7% of body weight if overweight, doing 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week (like brisk walking), reducing refined carbohydrates and sugary drinks, and improving sleep quality. In some cases, doctors also prescribe metformin as a preventive measure.

Prediabetes often has no symptoms — which is why routine screening matters. If caught early, many people bring their glucose back into the normal range entirely through lifestyle changes.

Glucose vs. HbA1c — what's the difference?

Fasting glucose gives you a snapshot of your blood sugar right now. HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin) shows your average blood sugar over the past 2–3 months. Together they give a much more complete picture.

  • Fasting glucose — point-in-time measurement, affected by recent diet, stress, illness
  • HbA1c — long-term average, not affected by what you ate yesterday
  • Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) — measures how your blood sugar responds to a sugary drink over 2 hours; used to diagnose gestational diabetes
  • Random glucose — taken any time of day without fasting; mainly used when diabetes symptoms are severe
What can raise fasting glucose?

Beyond diet and diabetes, several factors can temporarily or chronically elevate fasting glucose. Stress — both physical and emotional — triggers cortisol release, which raises blood sugar. Poor sleep consistently elevates fasting glucose even in healthy people. Certain medications raise blood sugar as a side effect, including corticosteroids, some antipsychotics, and certain blood pressure medications.

Chronic conditions that contribute to high glucose include polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), Cushing's syndrome, pancreatitis, and hypothyroidism. If your glucose is elevated but you don't have obvious risk factors for diabetes, your doctor may investigate these possibilities.

What to do next
A single fasting glucose result needs context. If it's in the prediabetes or diabetes range, your doctor will likely order a repeat test and an HbA1c before making any diagnosis. Don't panic — elevated glucose is one of the most manageable findings in medicine, especially when caught early. Focus on what you can control: diet, exercise, sleep, and stress. Only your doctor can interpret your specific result in context of your full health picture.

Frequently asked questions
Can one high glucose result diagnose diabetes?
No. According to the American Diabetes Association, a diabetes diagnosis requires either two separate fasting glucose readings of 126 mg/dL or higher, or one fasting glucose of 126+ confirmed by another type of test (like HbA1c or a glucose tolerance test). A single elevated reading on its own is not enough for a diagnosis.
What should I eat to lower my fasting glucose?
The most effective dietary changes are reducing refined carbohydrates (white bread, white rice, sugary drinks, pastries), increasing fiber intake (vegetables, legumes, whole grains), eating protein and healthy fats with meals to slow glucose absorption, and avoiding eating within 2–3 hours of bedtime. A registered dietitian can create a personalized plan based on your specific results and lifestyle.
Is 105 mg/dL fasting glucose bad?
A result of 105 mg/dL falls in the prediabetes range (100–125 mg/dL). It's not an emergency, but it is a meaningful signal that your blood sugar regulation needs attention. The good news is that at this level, lifestyle changes alone are often enough to bring glucose back into the normal range before it progresses further.
Can stress cause high fasting glucose?
Yes. When you're stressed, your body releases cortisol and adrenaline, which signal the liver to release stored glucose into the bloodstream. This is a survival mechanism — your body is preparing for "fight or flight." Chronic psychological stress can persistently elevate fasting glucose even without any dietary cause. Managing stress through exercise, sleep, and relaxation techniques can meaningfully improve blood sugar control.
Does coffee affect fasting glucose?
Black coffee can slightly raise blood sugar in some people due to caffeine's effect on insulin sensitivity. The effect is usually small and temporary, but if you're borderline prediabetic and testing for the first time, your doctor may ask you to skip coffee before the draw for the most accurate baseline result.
