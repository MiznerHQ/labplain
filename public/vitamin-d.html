Vitamin D Blood Test

What your results mean, what causes deficiency, and when to talk to your doctor.

What Is the Vitamin D Test?

The Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D) test measures the main form of vitamin D circulating in your blood. It's the best indicator of your overall vitamin D status.

Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption, bone strength, immune function, and muscle health. Most people get it from sunlight and fortified foods, but deficiency is extremely common — especially in northern climates, older adults, and people with darker skin.

Results are reported in nanograms per milliliter (ng/mL) or nanomoles per liter (nmol/L). This page uses ng/mL.

Normal Vitamin D Ranges

Most labs and medical organizations use these reference categories for adults:

CategoryLevel (ng/mL)Status
DeficientBelow 20Deficient
Insufficient20 – 29Insufficient
Sufficient30 – 39Sufficient
Optimal40 – 60Optimal
Elevated / Toxicity RiskAbove 100Too High

Reference ranges vary slightly by lab. Always compare your result to the range printed on your own report.

Symptoms of Abnormal Vitamin D

Deficiency usually develops silently over months, but can cause a range of physical and mental symptoms.

⬇ Low Vitamin D (Deficiency)

  • Fatigue and low energy
  • Bone pain or tenderness
  • Muscle weakness or cramps
  • Frequent infections or illness
  • Depression or low mood
  • Brain fog or poor concentration
  • Hair loss
  • Poor sleep quality

⬆ High Vitamin D (Toxicity)

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Excessive thirst or urination
  • Weakness and confusion
  • Kidney stones or kidney damage
  • Abnormal heart rhythm
  • Calcium buildup in soft tissues

What Causes Abnormal Vitamin D Levels?

Causes of Low Vitamin D

  • Limited sun exposure — spending most time indoors or living at northern latitudes (above 35°N) dramatically reduces production
  • Darker skin pigmentation — higher melanin reduces the skin's ability to synthesize vitamin D from sunlight
  • Older age — skin becomes less efficient at producing vitamin D with age
  • Obesity — vitamin D gets sequestered in body fat, reducing circulating levels
  • Malabsorption conditions — Crohn's disease, celiac disease, and bariatric surgery impair fat-soluble vitamin absorption
  • Kidney or liver disease — both organs are needed to convert vitamin D into its active form
  • Strict vegan diet — few plant foods contain meaningful amounts of vitamin D
  • Certain medications — anticonvulsants, glucocorticoids, and some antifungals can lower levels

Causes of High Vitamin D

  • Over-supplementation — by far the most common cause; taking high-dose supplements without monitoring
  • Granulomatous diseases — sarcoidosis and tuberculosis can cause the body to produce excess active vitamin D
  • Certain lymphomas — some cancers independently activate vitamin D
  • Excessive fortified food intake — rare but possible with extreme consumption

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get enough Vitamin D from sunlight alone?
Maybe — but it depends on where you live, your skin tone, age, and time outdoors. People in northern latitudes produce almost no vitamin D from sunlight between October and March, making supplementation essential for many people during winter months.
Is Vitamin D3 better than D2?
Yes, for most purposes. Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) raises blood levels more effectively and maintains them longer than D2 (ergocalciferol). Most doctors now recommend D3 when treating deficiency.
How long does it take to correct a deficiency?
With supplementation, it typically takes 3–6 months to restore levels to the optimal range. Your doctor may recommend a higher loading dose initially, followed by a lower maintenance dose. Retesting after 3 months is standard.
Can Vitamin D be toxic?
Yes — but only from supplements, not from sun exposure. Toxicity typically occurs above 100 ng/mL and can cause dangerous calcium buildup, kidney damage, and heart problems. This is why testing before starting high-dose supplementation matters.
Should I take Vitamin D with food?
Yes. Vitamin D is fat-soluble, meaning it absorbs significantly better when taken with a meal that contains fat. Taking it with breakfast or your largest meal of the day is ideal.
Does Vitamin D affect mood and depression?
Research suggests a link between low vitamin D and depression, seasonal affective disorder, and low mood — though the relationship is still being studied. Many people report improved energy and mood after correcting a deficiency.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Bring this list to your next appointment:

  • Is my level low enough to need prescription-strength treatment?
  • What dose of Vitamin D3 do you recommend for my specific result?
  • Should I also check my calcium, PTH, and magnesium?
  • How soon should I retest after starting supplementation?
  • Could a medication I'm taking be affecting my Vitamin D levels?
  • Is there an underlying condition causing my deficiency?
  • Should I be concerned about bone density given my level?
