ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate)

Your body's inflammation signal — what this test measures, what abnormal results mean, and when to take action.

What Is ESR?

The erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) measures how quickly your red blood cells sink to the bottom of a test tube over one hour. When inflammation is present in the body, certain proteins cause red blood cells to clump together and fall faster — resulting in a higher ESR reading.

Why Doctors Order It

ESR is a broad-based inflammation marker. It doesn't pinpoint where inflammation is coming from, but it helps doctors detect that something is wrong, monitor known conditions like rheumatoid arthritis or lupus, or track whether treatment is working. It's often ordered alongside CRP (C-reactive protein) for a fuller picture.

How It's Measured

A standard blood draw. Results are reported in millimeters per hour (mm/hr). The test is inexpensive, widely available, and has been used in medicine for over a century.

Symptoms Associated With High ESR

A high ESR doesn't cause symptoms directly — it reflects inflammation happening elsewhere. These are common symptoms that accompany the conditions driving a high ESR:

  • Persistent fatigue or feeling generally unwell
  • Unexplained fever or low-grade temperature
  • Joint pain, stiffness, or swelling
  • Muscle aches or tenderness (especially in the shoulders/hips)
  • Headaches, particularly new or worsening ones
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Rashes or skin changes
  • Prolonged illness that isn't improving

What Causes Abnormal ESR?

🔴 High ESR — Common Causes

  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Lupus (SLE)
  • Polymyalgia rheumatica
  • Giant cell arteritis
  • Infections (bacterial, viral)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Thyroid disease
  • Kidney disease
  • Anemia
  • Certain cancers (lymphoma, myeloma)

🔵 Low ESR — What It Means

  • Polycythemia vera (too many red blood cells)
  • Sickle cell disease
  • High dose corticosteroid use
  • Congestive heart failure
  • Hypofibrinogenemia
  • Extreme leukocytosis

⚠️ Non-Disease Factors That Raise ESR

  • Pregnancy
  • Older age
  • Female sex (naturally higher baseline)
  • Obesity
  • Recent surgery

📋 Often Ordered With

  • CRP (C-reactive protein)
  • CBC (complete blood count)
  • ANA (antinuclear antibody)
  • Rheumatoid factor (RF)
  • Ferritin
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel

Frequently Asked Questions

My ESR is slightly elevated. Should I be worried?

A mildly elevated ESR — especially in older adults or women — doesn't always mean something serious. Many people have slightly elevated ESRs without any significant disease. Context matters: your doctor will weigh your symptoms, medical history, and other lab values before drawing conclusions.

What's the difference between ESR and CRP?

Both measure inflammation, but CRP responds faster — it rises within hours of inflammation and drops quickly when it resolves. ESR is slower to rise and fall, taking days to weeks. CRP is better for detecting acute infection; ESR is better for monitoring chronic inflammatory conditions over time.

Can ESR be elevated without any disease?

Yes. Pregnancy, older age, obesity, and female biology can all push ESR above the standard reference range with no underlying disease present. That's why ESR is always interpreted alongside clinical findings — not in isolation.

A very high ESR — above 100 mm/hr — what does that mean?

An ESR over 100 mm/hr is considered markedly elevated and warrants urgent investigation. Common causes include serious infection (like osteomyelitis or endocarditis), giant cell arteritis, multiple myeloma, or vasculitis. Your doctor will likely order additional tests immediately.

How do I lower a high ESR?

You treat the underlying cause. If inflammation is from an autoimmune condition, medications like NSAIDs, steroids, or disease-modifying drugs reduce it. If from infection, antibiotics or antivirals address the source. As the underlying problem resolves, ESR typically normalizes.

Does diet affect ESR?

Indirectly. An anti-inflammatory diet (rich in omega-3s, vegetables, and low in processed foods) can reduce systemic inflammation over time. Obesity also raises ESR, so weight management helps. But diet alone won't normalize a significantly elevated ESR driven by disease.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  1. My ESR is [X] — is that mildly or significantly outside the normal range for my age and sex?
  2. What do you think is causing this elevated ESR based on my symptoms and history?
  3. Should I also get a CRP test to compare the two inflammation markers?
  4. Do I need to see a rheumatologist, or can this be managed by my primary care doctor?
  5. Will we retest ESR in a few weeks to see if it changes?
  6. What warning signs or symptoms should prompt me to call your office before my next appointment?
