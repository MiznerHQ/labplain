What does your
Calcium result mean?

Minerals

What this test measures

A calcium blood test measures the amount of calcium circulating in your bloodstream. Calcium is one of the most important minerals in the body — essential for strong bones and teeth, but also critical for muscle contraction, nerve signaling, blood clotting, and heart rhythm.

About 99% of your body's calcium is stored in bones and teeth. The remaining 1% circulates in the blood and tissues, where it helps your body function normally. Your body tightly controls calcium levels using hormones from the parathyroid glands, vitamin D, and the kidneys.

Doctors commonly order calcium testing as part of a comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP) or when investigating symptoms like fatigue, muscle cramps, kidney stones, abnormal heart rhythms, or bone problems.

Normal reference range
Total Calcium: 8.5–10.5 mg/dL

Normal ranges vary slightly between laboratories depending on the equipment and testing methods used. Always compare your result to the reference range printed on your specific lab report.

Calcium levels can also be affected by albumin, a blood protein that binds calcium. If albumin is abnormal, your doctor may calculate a "corrected calcium" level or order an ionized calcium test for a more accurate picture.

🩸 Total vs ionized calcium

Most standard blood tests measure total calcium, which includes calcium attached to proteins and free calcium. Ionized calcium measures only the biologically active form and may be more accurate in critically ill patients or when albumin levels are abnormal.

What your result might indicate
↑ If High

High calcium levels (hypercalcemia) may be caused by overactive parathyroid glands, certain cancers, dehydration, excessive vitamin D supplementation, or some medications. Mild elevations may not cause symptoms, but severe elevations can become dangerous.

↓ If Low

Low calcium levels (hypocalcemia) may result from vitamin D deficiency, kidney disease, low magnesium, hypoparathyroidism, or poor nutrition. Significant low calcium can affect muscles, nerves, and heart function.

Symptoms associated with abnormal calcium

↑ High Calcium Symptoms

  • Excessive thirst and frequent urination
  • Kidney stones
  • Nausea or constipation
  • Abdominal pain
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Confusion or brain fog
  • Bone pain
  • Abnormal heart rhythms

↓ Low Calcium Symptoms

  • Muscle cramps or spasms
  • Tingling in fingers or around the mouth
  • Numbness
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Muscle twitching
  • Seizures in severe cases
  • Dry skin or brittle nails
  • Abnormal heart rhythms
Common causes of abnormal calcium

What can raise calcium?

The most common cause of high calcium is hyperparathyroidism — a condition where one or more parathyroid glands produce too much parathyroid hormone (PTH). Certain cancers can also raise calcium, especially cancers involving the bones or hormone-producing tumors.

Other causes include dehydration, excessive calcium or vitamin D supplements, prolonged bed rest, certain medications like thiazide diuretics, and rare endocrine disorders. Mild elevations are sometimes found incidentally during routine blood work.

What can lower calcium?

Low calcium is commonly caused by vitamin D deficiency, chronic kidney disease, low magnesium levels, or hypoparathyroidism — where the parathyroid glands don't produce enough hormone to regulate calcium properly.

Low calcium can also occur in severe illness, pancreatitis, malnutrition, alcoholism, or after major surgery. Some medications, including bisphosphonates and certain chemotherapy drugs, may also lower calcium levels.

Tests often ordered alongside calcium

Calcium levels are usually interpreted alongside other lab tests, including:

  • Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) — helps determine whether the parathyroid glands are causing the problem
  • Vitamin D — low vitamin D is a common cause of low calcium
  • Albumin — used to calculate corrected calcium levels
  • Magnesium — magnesium helps regulate calcium balance
  • Phosphorus — calcium and phosphorus are closely linked in bone metabolism
What to do next
A mildly abnormal calcium level doesn't always mean something serious. Dehydration, supplements, medications, and temporary illnesses can all affect calcium levels. Your doctor will usually interpret your result alongside albumin, kidney function, vitamin D, and parathyroid hormone levels before deciding whether further testing is needed.
Questions to ask your doctor
  • 01Is my calcium level only mildly abnormal or significantly outside the normal range?
  • 02Should my calcium be corrected based on my albumin level?
  • 03Do I need a parathyroid hormone (PTH) test or vitamin D test?
  • 04Could supplements or medications be affecting my calcium level?
  • 05Do my symptoms fit with high or low calcium?
  • 06Should my calcium level be repeated or monitored over time?
Frequently asked questions
Does high calcium always mean cancer?
No. The most common cause of high calcium is hyperparathyroidism, not cancer. Mild elevations are often caused by benign conditions, dehydration, or supplements. Your doctor will look at the overall pattern of your labs and symptoms before drawing conclusions.
Can dehydration affect calcium levels?
Yes. Dehydration can temporarily raise calcium levels because the blood becomes more concentrated. Rehydration may normalize mildly elevated results.
Should I stop taking calcium supplements if my level is high?
Don't stop prescribed supplements without talking to your doctor first. However, excessive calcium supplementation can contribute to high calcium levels, so your doctor may review your dose and any vitamin D supplements you're taking.
What's the difference between total calcium and ionized calcium?
Total calcium measures all calcium in the blood, including calcium attached to proteins. Ionized calcium measures only the active form your body actually uses. Ionized calcium is sometimes more accurate in people with abnormal protein levels or critical illness.
Can low vitamin D cause low calcium?
Yes. Vitamin D helps your body absorb calcium from food. If vitamin D is too low, calcium absorption drops, which can eventually lead to low calcium levels.
