What does your
Uric Acid result mean?

Metabolic
⚠️ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.
What this test measures

Uric acid is a waste product your body makes when it breaks down purines — natural compounds found in many foods and in your own cells. Normally, uric acid dissolves in the blood, passes through the kidneys, and leaves the body in urine. When your body produces too much or your kidneys excrete too little, uric acid builds up in the bloodstream.

That buildup is called hyperuricemia. When uric acid levels stay elevated long enough, it can crystallize and deposit in joints — causing gout — or in the kidneys — causing kidney stones. A serum uric acid blood test measures how much is currently circulating in your blood.

Doctors order this test when evaluating gout, kidney stones, kidney disease, or conditions that involve rapid cell turnover (like certain cancers or their treatments).

Normal reference range
Men: 3.4–7.0 mg/dL  |  Women: 2.4–6.0 mg/dL

Reference ranges differ between men and women because estrogen helps the kidneys excrete uric acid more efficiently — so women naturally run lower. After menopause, women's levels rise and approach men's. Results above 7.0 mg/dL (men) or 6.0 mg/dL (women) are considered elevated.

Labs may define ranges slightly differently. Always compare your result to the reference range printed on your specific lab report, not a generic internet number.

💧 Hydration and diet matter

Dehydration, fasting, and a high-purine meal the day before testing can temporarily raise uric acid levels. If your result is borderline, your doctor may ask you to repeat the test under more controlled conditions.

What your result might indicate
↑ If High

Elevated uric acid (hyperuricemia) may point to gout, kidney disease, dehydration, a high-purine diet, metabolic syndrome, or certain medications. High levels alone don't always cause symptoms — some people have elevated uric acid for years with no problems.

↓ If Low

Low uric acid (hypouricemia) is less common and usually not a health concern on its own. It can occur with certain liver conditions, rare genetic disorders (like Fanconi syndrome), or with medications such as high-dose aspirin or certain diuretics.

Symptoms associated with abnormal uric acid

↑ High Uric Acid Symptoms

  • Sudden, intense joint pain (especially big toe)
  • Swollen, red, warm joints
  • Tenderness that worsens with touch
  • Flaking or peeling skin over affected joint
  • Tophi (uric acid lumps under skin)
  • Kidney stone pain (flank, back, groin)
  • Nausea or blood in urine (with stones)
  • Frequent or painful urination

↓ Low Uric Acid Symptoms

  • Often no symptoms at all
  • Fatigue (if related to underlying condition)
  • Muscle weakness (rare genetic causes)
  • Signs of kidney dysfunction
  • Neurological symptoms (rare, genetic)
Common causes of abnormal uric acid

What can raise uric acid?

Diet is a major driver — foods high in purines include red meat, organ meats (liver, kidney), shellfish, anchovies, sardines, and beer (especially dark beer). Fructose-sweetened beverages like soda have also been strongly linked to elevated uric acid. Beyond diet, the kidneys failing to excrete uric acid efficiently — due to kidney disease, dehydration, or genetics — is the most common underlying cause.

Certain medications raise uric acid levels, particularly diuretics (water pills) used for blood pressure, low-dose aspirin, niacin, and some immunosuppressants. Rapid cell breakdown from chemotherapy, radiation, or conditions like psoriasis can also flood the blood with purines and spike uric acid suddenly.

What can lower uric acid?

Low uric acid is uncommon. It can result from rare genetic disorders that affect purine metabolism, liver disease (since uric acid is produced partly in the liver), or medications like high-dose aspirin and certain diuretics at specific doses. In most cases, low uric acid is an incidental finding and not clinically significant on its own.

Tests often ordered alongside uric acid

Uric acid is rarely looked at in isolation. Your doctor may also order:

  • BMP or CMP (Basic/Comprehensive Metabolic Panel) — checks kidney function, electrolytes, and glucose alongside uric acid
  • Creatinine and BUN — key kidney function markers that help explain why uric acid may be elevated
  • CBC (Complete Blood Count) — can detect blood disorders or rapid cell turnover
  • Joint fluid analysis (arthrocentesis) — gold standard for confirming gout by identifying uric acid crystals under a microscope
  • 24-hour urine uric acid — determines whether the problem is overproduction or under-excretion
  • Lipid panel — gout and high uric acid often co-occur with metabolic syndrome and high triglycerides
What to do next
An elevated uric acid result is a signal worth paying attention to, but it doesn't automatically mean you have gout or that you'll develop it. Many people have high uric acid with no symptoms. What matters is the trend over time, your symptoms, your kidney function, and your risk factors. Work with your doctor to understand whether lifestyle changes, medication, or simply monitoring is the right path for you.
Questions to ask your doctor
  • 01Is my uric acid level high enough to require treatment, or should we just monitor it?
  • 02Do I have signs of gout, kidney stones, or kidney damage related to this result?
  • 03Could any of my current medications be contributing to elevated uric acid?
  • 04What dietary changes would make the biggest difference in my specific case?
  • 05Should I check a 24-hour urine uric acid to see if I'm overproducing or under-excreting?
  • 06If I do need medication (like allopurinol), what are the risks and benefits?
Frequently asked questions
Can you have gout with a normal uric acid level?
Yes — and this surprises many people. During an acute gout attack, uric acid can temporarily drop to normal as crystals are being deposited in the joints. A normal blood level during a flare doesn't rule out gout. Joint fluid analysis, which looks for uric acid crystals directly, is the definitive test.
Does high uric acid always cause gout?
No. Many people have persistently elevated uric acid (hyperuricemia) and never develop gout or kidney stones. However, the higher and more prolonged the elevation, the greater the risk. Other factors like genetics, kidney function, and hydration also play a role in whether symptoms develop.
Is beer really that much worse than other alcohol for uric acid?
Yes — beer is particularly problematic because it contains both alcohol (which reduces uric acid excretion) and purines from the brewing process. Wine has less of an effect, and spirits are somewhere in between. But all alcohol raises uric acid to some degree, and people prone to gout are generally advised to minimize alcohol intake overall.
What's the uric acid target if I'm being treated for gout?
Most guidelines recommend keeping uric acid below 6.0 mg/dL to prevent crystal formation and reduce gout flares. For people with frequent attacks or tophi (uric acid deposits under the skin), a target below 5.0 mg/dL is often recommended. Your doctor will set a specific target based on your history.
Can drinking more water lower my uric acid?
Hydration helps the kidneys filter and excrete uric acid more efficiently, so staying well-hydrated is consistently recommended for people with elevated levels. It won't dramatically lower very high uric acid on its own, but it's a low-effort step that helps — and may reduce kidney stone risk significantly.
