Ferritin Blood Test

Your body's iron storage protein — and a critical clue for fatigue, anemia, and inflammation.

What Is Ferritin?

Ferritin is a protein that stores iron inside your cells and releases it when your body needs it. A ferritin blood test measures how much iron your body has in reserve — making it one of the most important markers for diagnosing iron-deficiency anemia and iron overload.

Unlike serum iron, which reflects iron currently circulating in your blood, ferritin shows your total iron stores. You can have normal serum iron but critically depleted ferritin — meaning your reserves are nearly gone. This is why doctors often check ferritin alongside other iron studies.

Ferritin is also an acute-phase reactant, meaning it rises during inflammation, infection, liver disease, and certain cancers — so high ferritin doesn't always mean too much iron. Context matters enormously.

Symptoms by Ferritin Level

🔵 Low Ferritin Symptoms

  • Persistent fatigue and weakness
  • Hair thinning or hair loss
  • Restless legs syndrome (especially at night)
  • Brittle nails or spoon-shaped nails
  • Shortness of breath on exertion
  • Brain fog, difficulty concentrating
  • Cold hands and feet
  • Pale inner eyelids or skin

🔴 High Ferritin Symptoms

  • Joint pain (especially knuckles and hips)
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Abdominal pain
  • Bronze or gray skin discoloration
  • Reduced libido or sexual dysfunction
  • Heart palpitations or irregular heartbeat
  • Elevated blood sugar
  • Often asymptomatic early on

Common Causes of Abnormal Ferritin

Causes of Low Ferritin

  • Inadequate dietary iron: Vegetarian and vegan diets are high risk; plant-based iron (non-heme) is absorbed less efficiently than meat-based iron.
  • Heavy menstrual periods: The most common cause in women of reproductive age — chronic blood loss exceeds iron intake.
  • GI blood loss: Ulcers, polyps, celiac disease, Crohn's, or colon cancer can cause slow, invisible blood loss that drains iron stores.
  • Malabsorption: Celiac disease, atrophic gastritis, or post-bariatric surgery impairs iron absorption.
  • Pregnancy: Increased iron demands for the fetus can deplete maternal stores.
  • Frequent blood donation: Donors who give repeatedly without iron supplementation are at risk.

Causes of High Ferritin

  • Hereditary hemochromatosis: A genetic condition causing excessive iron absorption — the most common serious genetic disease in people of Northern European descent.
  • Chronic inflammation: Rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, IBD, and other inflammatory conditions elevate ferritin as an acute-phase response — not true iron overload.
  • Liver disease: Hepatitis, fatty liver disease, and cirrhosis cause ferritin to leak from damaged liver cells into the bloodstream.
  • Metabolic syndrome and obesity: Associated with elevated ferritin even without true iron overload.
  • Alcoholism: Increases ferritin through both liver damage and increased iron absorption.
  • Multiple blood transfusions: Each unit of blood delivers additional iron.
  • Certain cancers: Leukemia, lymphoma, and some solid tumors can dramatically elevate ferritin.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I have iron deficiency if my ferritin is "in range"?

Yes. Labs define "normal" as 12 ng/mL and above, but many people experience symptoms of iron deficiency — especially fatigue and hair loss — when ferritin falls below 30–50 ng/mL. If your ferritin is low-normal and you have symptoms, it's worth discussing with your doctor.

My ferritin is high but I feel fine. Should I be worried?

High ferritin often has no symptoms in early stages. It's important to determine the cause — your doctor will likely check for inflammation markers, liver function, and may order genetic testing for hemochromatosis. High ferritin from inflammation is different from true iron overload, which requires treatment.

What's the difference between ferritin and serum iron?

Serum iron measures the iron actively circulating in your blood right now. Ferritin measures your stored reserves. You can have normal serum iron but very low ferritin (your reserves are nearly gone), or high serum iron but low ferritin (rapid turnover). That's why doctors often order the full iron panel together.

How do I raise low ferritin?

Dietary iron (red meat, liver, dark leafy greens, lentils), iron supplements, and treating the underlying cause (e.g., stopping GI bleeding). Pairing iron-rich foods with vitamin C improves absorption. Avoid calcium, coffee, and tea near iron intake. Severe deficiency may require IV iron infusions.

How do I lower high ferritin?

Treatment depends on the cause. True iron overload (hemochromatosis) is treated with therapeutic phlebotomy — regular blood draws to remove excess iron. Inflammation-related high ferritin requires treating the underlying condition. Reducing alcohol intake and dietary red meat may help modestly.

Does ferritin go up with infection or illness?

Yes. Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant and can spike dramatically during acute infections, severe illness, or inflammatory flares. A high ferritin result after a recent illness may not reflect your true iron status — retest after recovery if your doctor recommends it.
