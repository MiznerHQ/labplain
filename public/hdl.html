What does your
HDL Cholesterol result mean?

Heart Health
⚠️ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.
What this test measures

HDL (high-density lipoprotein) is known as "good" cholesterol because it works in reverse from LDL — it picks up excess cholesterol from your arteries and carries it back to the liver to be removed from the body. Higher HDL levels are generally protective against heart disease. Low HDL is an independent risk factor for heart attack and stroke, even if your LDL looks normal.

Normal reference range
Men: 40+ mg/dL (optimal 60+)  |  Women: 50+ mg/dL (optimal 60+)
↑ High (Protective)

60 mg/dL or above is considered cardioprotective. Very high HDL (above 100) may occasionally signal a rare genetic condition — worth discussing with your doctor.

↓ Low (Increased Risk)

Below 40 mg/dL (men) or 50 mg/dL (women) raises cardiovascular risk. Below 35 mg/dL is considered dangerously low regardless of gender.

Reference ranges vary by lab. Always compare to the range printed on your own lab report.

Symptoms & Signs

Low HDL has no direct symptoms on its own. Like high LDL, it silently increases cardiovascular risk over years. Symptoms only appear once damage has occurred:

Chest pain or pressureReduced HDL allows plaque to build in coronary arteries, restricting blood flow.
Shortness of breathMay indicate early heart or circulatory stress from years of low HDL.
Fatigue during activityPoor cardiovascular efficiency from arterial narrowing linked to low HDL.
No symptoms at allMost people with low HDL feel completely normal — making routine testing essential.

Common Causes of Low HDL

Lifestyle factors
  • Physical inactivity
  • Smoking or tobacco use
  • Diet high in refined carbs and sugar
  • Obesity, especially abdominal fat
  • Heavy alcohol use
Medical conditions
  • Type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance
  • Metabolic syndrome
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Certain medications (beta-blockers, steroids)

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you raise HDL naturally?
Yes. Regular aerobic exercise is the most effective way — it can raise HDL by 5–10%. Quitting smoking, losing weight, and eating healthy fats (olive oil, avocado, nuts) also help. HDL is harder to raise than LDL is to lower, but lifestyle changes do work.
Is there medication to raise HDL?
There's no approved drug specifically for raising HDL. Niacin raises HDL but clinical trials haven't shown it reduces heart events. Statins and fibrates modestly improve HDL as a side effect. Lifestyle changes remain the primary approach.
Can HDL be too high?
Very high HDL (above 80–100 mg/dL) may paradoxically increase inflammation in some people. Recent research suggests extremely high HDL may not always be protective. If your HDL is unusually high, your doctor may investigate further.
What's the HDL-to-LDL ratio?
Some doctors use the LDL:HDL ratio as a risk indicator. A ratio below 3.5 is generally considered healthy. A high ratio means your bad cholesterol far outweighs the good — a stronger predictor of risk than either number alone.
Does gender affect HDL levels?
Yes. Women naturally have higher HDL than men due to estrogen, which is why the healthy threshold is higher for women (50 mg/dL vs 40 mg/dL for men). After menopause, women's HDL levels often drop.
