🤔 What Is ALT?

ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) is an enzyme found mainly in liver cells. When liver cells are damaged or inflamed, they release ALT into the bloodstream — so a blood test can detect the spill. It's one of the most specific markers for liver health and is almost always included in a comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP).

ALT is often measured alongside AST (another liver enzyme). The ratio between the two helps doctors distinguish between different types of liver disease.

⚠️ Symptoms of High ALT

Mildly elevated ALT often causes no symptoms at all. When symptoms do appear, they usually indicate more significant liver stress:

Fatigue

Persistent tiredness or low energy, even with adequate sleep.

Abdominal Discomfort

Dull ache or fullness in the upper right side of the abdomen.

Jaundice

Yellowing of the skin or whites of the eyes — a serious sign.

Nausea or Poor Appetite

Feeling queasy, especially after meals or with fatty foods.

Dark Urine

Urine that looks brown or tea-colored can indicate liver stress.

Easy Bruising

The liver produces clotting proteins — damage can affect this.

🔍 Common Causes of High ALT

  • Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD): The most common cause in Western countries. Fat accumulation in the liver triggers inflammation and enzyme release.
  • Alcohol Use: Regular or heavy alcohol consumption damages liver cells directly, causing ALT to leak into the blood.
  • Viral Hepatitis (A, B, or C): Hepatitis viruses infect and inflame liver tissue, dramatically raising ALT levels.
  • Medications: Statins, acetaminophen (Tylenol), NSAIDs, antibiotics, and some supplements can elevate ALT — even at normal doses in some people.
  • Thyroid Disorders: Both hypothyroidism and hyperthyroidism can mildly elevate liver enzymes.
  • Celiac Disease: Undiagnosed gluten intolerance can cause mild liver enzyme elevation that resolves with a gluten-free diet.
  • Strenuous Exercise: Intense workouts can briefly raise ALT (and AST), particularly in athletes.
  • Autoimmune Hepatitis: The immune system mistakenly attacks liver cells, causing chronic inflammation.

💊 Causes of Low ALT

Low ALT is usually not a concern. However, very low levels have been associated with:

  • Vitamin B6 deficiency — ALT requires B6 to function; deficiency lowers enzyme activity.
  • Advanced liver disease or cirrhosis — when so few liver cells remain that enzyme output drops.
  • Older age — ALT naturally tends to decrease with aging.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

My ALT is slightly above normal. Should I panic?

Not necessarily. A mildly elevated ALT (just above the upper limit) is common and can result from recent exercise, a heavy meal, a new medication, or even normal lab variation. Your doctor will likely retest in a few weeks to see if it normalizes.

How is ALT different from AST?

Both are liver enzymes, but ALT is more specific to the liver. AST is also found in muscle, heart, and other tissues. If both are elevated, the ALT/AST ratio helps narrow down the cause — a ratio above 2:1 often suggests alcohol-related liver disease.

Can I lower my ALT without medication?

Yes, in many cases. Reducing alcohol intake, losing weight if overweight, avoiding unnecessary supplements or medications that stress the liver, and eating a balanced diet can all help bring ALT down over time — especially with NAFLD.

What level of ALT requires immediate attention?

ALT above 200–300 U/L warrants prompt evaluation. Levels in the thousands suggest acute liver injury — from a medication overdose, acute hepatitis, or other serious cause — and require urgent medical care.

Will my ALT be affected by what I ate before the test?

A large fatty meal can mildly affect results. For the most accurate reading, most labs recommend fasting for 8–12 hours before a comprehensive metabolic panel.
