What does your
eGFR result mean?

Kidney Function
⚠ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.
What this test measures

eGFR stands for Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate. It tracks how efficiently your kidneys are filtering waste products from your bloodstream. Your kidneys contain millions of microscopic filtering units called glomeruli; your eGFR calculation directly reflects how well these filters are performing.

Instead of looking at a single metric, your doctor looks at your eGFR score alongside a chemical waste product called creatinine (and sometimes cystatin C) to estimate your overall kidney capacity. Your final eGFR score changes based on your blood results, age, and assigned biological sex.

A routine eGFR check is standard practice during annual physicals, but it's especially critical if you are managing chronic health conditions like high blood pressure or type 2 diabetes, both of which can progressively strain kidney function over time.

Normal reference range
Normal Kidney Function: 90 mL/min/1.73m² or higher

For adults, an eGFR reading above 90 is considered fully normal. However, a slight, progressive decline is natural as we age. A score between 60 and 89 can still be entirely healthy if there is no other evidence of structural kidney damage.

Because an eGFR is a mathematical estimate rather than a direct measurement, minor day-to-day variances are normal. Always consult the exact reference ranges provided directly on your lab printout.

⏰ Why hydration matters

Unlike other metabolic markers, the time of day does not change your eGFR score. However, acute dehydration will temporarily reduce your kidney blood flow, causing a false low reading on your test. Ensure you are normally hydrated before your blood draw.

What your result might indicate
↑ If Normal/High (90+)

An eGFR above 90 signifies strong, healthy filtration. There is no upper ceiling limit for safety, though remarkably high scores can occasionally be triggered by high dietary protein or a surge in muscle mass.

↓ If Low (Below 60)

A score persistently under 60 indicates reduced filtration capacity. If this reduction lasts for more than three months, it points to Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) and requires further medical evaluation.

Understanding the 5 CKD Stages

When an eGFR result falls below normal parameters, physicians utilize a standardized 5-stage scale to classify kidney health:

  • Stage 1 (eGFR 90+): Normal filtration efficiency, but alternative markers (like protein in urine) indicate mild kidney stress or structural damage.
  • Stage 2 (eGFR 60–89): A mild decrease in filtration speed. Often seen as a normal part of aging, but monitored carefully if pre-existing risk factors exist.
  • Stage 3 (eGFR 30–59): Moderate kidney dysfunction. This stage is frequently split into 3a (45-59) and 3b (30-44). Medical management begins here to actively preserve remaining function.
  • Stage 4 (eGFR 15–29): Severe reduction in function. The kidneys are struggling significantly to process everyday waste, necessitating close care from a nephrologist.
  • Stage 5 (eGFR below 15): Advanced kidney failure. At this stage, the organs can no longer sustain systemic health on their own, requiring dialysis treatment or a transplant plan.
Symptoms associated with abnormal eGFR

↑ Normal / High eGFR Symptoms

  • Excellent baseline energy levels
  • Normal fluid balance throughout the body
  • Steady, controlled blood pressure
  • Standard, clear urinary patterns
  • No unprovoked physical swelling

↓ Low eGFR Symptoms (Stages 3-5)

  • Swelling (edema) in the ankles, feet, or legs
  • Persistent fatigue and low physical endurance
  • Changes in urination frequency or foamy urine
  • Dry, itchy skin or muscle cramping
  • Shortness of breath or systemic fluid retention
  • Metallic taste in the mouth or loss of appetite
Common causes of abnormal filtration

What maintains a normal eGFR?

A consistently normal eGFR is supported by robust cardiovascular health, controlled blood sugar, and clear arterial pathways to the kidneys. Regular physical activity, a balanced diet low in heavily processed sodium, and maintaining optimal hydration all protect your delicate kidney filtration networks from premature degradation.

What can lower your eGFR?

The leading driver of a chronically declining eGFR is uncontrolled type 2 diabetes, as prolonged high blood sugar strips away the integrity of the kidney's micro-filters. Long-standing high blood pressure acts similarly by physically scarring the renal blood vessels.

Sudden, acute drops can be sparked by severe dehydration, acute kidney injuries (AKI), or the regular, unmonitored use of over-the-counter NSAID pain relievers like ibuprofen or naproxen, which limit essential blood flow to the kidneys.

Tests often ordered alongside eGFR

Because an eGFR calculation relies heavily on baseline metabolic metrics, doctors cross-reference it with adjacent panels:

  • Creatinine — The fundamental blood waste product generated by muscle breakdown that directly calculates your eGFR.
  • Urine Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR) — A targeted urine test that looks for early signs of protein leaks, indicating early structural kidney strain.
  • BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) — Tracks liver and muscle protein waste to evaluate how well your filtration system is purging metabolic byproducts.
  • Serum Potassium & Phosphorus — Vital electrolytes that can spike dangerously when your kidneys lose filtration capacity.
  • Kidney Ultrasound — An imaging tool utilized to look for physical blockages, kidney stones, or physical shrinking of the organs.
What to do next
A single isolated low eGFR reading does not mean you have chronic kidney failure. Kidney values jump around based on your hydration, food intake, and acute medications. A true medical diagnosis requires a trend of matching numbers sustained over 90 days. Keep calm, keep hydrating, and let your practitioner evaluate the multi-month trend lines.
Questions to ask your doctor
  • 01Does my current eGFR result suggest a specific stage of kidney disease?
  • 02Could temporary dehydration or an over-the-counter painkiller have dropped my score?
  • 03Is there any trace of protein or blood showing up in my routine urine tests?
  • 04Are my current blood pressure and blood sugar medications optimized to protect my kidneys?
  • 05Do you recommend adjusting my diet, protein intake, or fluid intake based on this number?
  • 06At what point or target score would you recommend I consult a specialist nephrologist?
Frequently asked questions
Can a low eGFR score improve or bounce back?
Yes. If the drop was caused by an acute issue like dehydration, a recent illness, or a medication interaction, your eGFR can recover completely once those factors are resolved. Chronic decline cannot be fully reversed, but it can be successfully paused or slowed down for decades with lifestyle changes.
Why does my age affect the eGFR calculation?
Our kidneys naturally lose a tiny fraction of their filtering capabilities every year as we age. The mathematical formula automatically adjusts for this reality so an older adult isn't misdiagnosed with kidney disease for showing a normal, age-appropriate decline.
Can a high-protein diet alter my eGFR result?
Yes. Consuming large amounts of cooked meat or whey protein supplements right before a blood test can temporarily boost the creatinine levels in your blood. This can lead to a falsely low eGFR score, even if your underlying kidney health is perfectly fine.
What is the difference between eGFR and a standard Creatinine test?
Creatinine is the raw waste molecule measured in your blood. The eGFR is the actual calculated score generated by running that raw creatinine number through a medical formula. The eGFR tells the real clinical story of your kidney health, whereas raw creatinine is harder to read on its own.
