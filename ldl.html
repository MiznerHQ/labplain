LDL Cholesterol

What your result means, what affects it, and what to do next — explained in plain English.

⚠️ Educational only. LabPlain does not provide medical advice or diagnosis. Always discuss your specific results with your healthcare provider.

🔬 What This Test Measures

LDL stands for low-density lipoprotein — commonly called "bad" cholesterol. It carries cholesterol from your liver through your bloodstream to your cells. When LDL levels are too high, excess cholesterol can deposit in artery walls, forming plaques that narrow and harden the arteries over time.

Unlike HDL (the "good" cholesterol that clears excess from your blood), high LDL is a major driver of atherosclerosis — the process behind most heart attacks and strokes.

LDL is measured as part of a lipid panel, typically after a 9–12 hour fast. It's reported in milligrams per deciliter (mg/dL).

📊 Normal Reference Ranges

These are the standard American Heart Association / ACC guidelines for adults:

LDL LevelCategory
Below 100 mg/dLOptimal
100–129 mg/dLNear Optimal
130–159 mg/dLBorderline High
160–189 mg/dLHigh
190 mg/dL and aboveVery High

⚠️ Your target LDL may be lower than these general ranges if you have diabetes, heart disease, or other cardiovascular risk factors. Always use the range on your own lab report and ask your doctor about your personal target.

⚡ What Your Result Might Indicate

↑ If High

  • Plaque buildup in arteries (atherosclerosis)
  • Increased risk of heart attack or stroke
  • Diet high in saturated or trans fats
  • Genetic high cholesterol (familial hypercholesterolemia)
  • Underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism)
  • Diabetes or insulin resistance
  • Kidney disease or nephrotic syndrome
  • Certain medications (steroids, some blood pressure drugs)
  • Sedentary lifestyle or obesity

↓ If Low

  • Generally considered favorable
  • Extremely low levels may indicate malnutrition
  • Overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism)
  • Certain genetic conditions (abetalipoproteinemia)
  • Liver disease affecting cholesterol production
  • Effect of cholesterol-lowering medications

🩺 Symptoms & Warning Signs

High LDL itself causes no symptoms — it's a silent risk factor. Most people have no idea their LDL is elevated until a blood test reveals it, or until a cardiovascular event occurs.

However, if LDL has been very high for many years, you may eventually develop:

  • Xanthomas — fatty deposits under the skin, often on elbows, knees, or tendons
  • Xanthelasmas — yellowish patches around the eyelids
  • Corneal arcus — a grayish ring around the edge of the cornea (more significant in people under 45)
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath if coronary artery disease has developed

This is why routine lipid panel screening is so important — catching high LDL early, before symptoms appear, is the goal.

⚠️ Risk Factors for High LDL

Diet & Lifestyle

  • High intake of saturated fat (red meat, full-fat dairy, butter)
  • Trans fats (partially hydrogenated oils in processed foods)
  • Low physical activity
  • Excess weight or obesity
  • Smoking (damages blood vessels and worsens cholesterol balance)

Medical Conditions

  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Liver disease
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

Genetics

  • Familial hypercholesterolemia — a genetic mutation causing very high LDL from birth, present in about 1 in 250 people
  • Family history of early heart disease (father before 55, mother before 65)

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between LDL and total cholesterol?

Total cholesterol is the sum of all cholesterol types in your blood — LDL, HDL, and VLDL. LDL is the specific fraction most strongly associated with heart disease risk. It's possible to have normal total cholesterol but high LDL, which is why LDL is tested separately.

Can I lower my LDL without medication?

Often, yes — especially for borderline-high levels. Reducing saturated fat and trans fats, increasing soluble fiber (oats, beans, apples), regular aerobic exercise, losing excess weight, and quitting smoking can all meaningfully lower LDL. However, people with very high LDL (especially genetic forms) almost always need medication too.

How much do statins lower LDL?

Statins are the most commonly prescribed cholesterol-lowering drugs. Depending on the statin and dose, they typically reduce LDL by 30–50%. High-intensity statins like rosuvastatin or atorvastatin at higher doses can lower LDL by over 50%.

Do I need to fast before an LDL test?

Traditionally yes — a 9–12 hour fast was recommended because eating raises triglycerides, which affects calculated LDL. Many modern labs now use direct LDL measurement that doesn't require fasting, but check with your doctor or lab beforehand.

What is a "calculated" vs "direct" LDL?

Most standard lipid panels calculate LDL using the Friedewald equation (Total Cholesterol − HDL − Triglycerides/5). Direct LDL measures it directly and is more accurate when triglycerides are very high. Your lab report will usually indicate which method was used.

Is LDL particle size important?

Yes — small, dense LDL particles are more dangerous than large, fluffy ones, even at the same LDL number. Advanced lipid testing (like LDL-P or apolipoprotein B) can assess this, but it's not part of a standard lipid panel. Ask your doctor if this is relevant for you.
