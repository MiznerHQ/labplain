LDL Cholesterol Test

Low-Density Lipoprotein (The "Bad" Cholesterol) — A key marker used to evaluate your cardiovascular health and arterial plaque buildup risk.

Optimal Range Below 100 mg/dL
High Value Risk 130+ mg/dL
Low Value Risk Below 40 mg/dL
Test Type Lipid Panel Panel

What is LDL Cholesterol?

LDL stands for Low-Density Lipoprotein. Think of it as a delivery vehicle transporting cholesterol molecules through your bloodstream. While your system requires standard amounts of cholesterol to properly build healthy cell walls, carrying an excess of these delivery vehicles across your circulation system leads to vascular traffic—specifically causing fatty deposits called plaque to compile along your inner artery walls.

Understanding Your Ranges

Reference evaluation configurations generally fluctuate marginally between separate clinical processing centers. Always verify your unique baseline results explicitly alongside the specific metrics assigned directly onto your official testing paperwork.

Result Level Clinical Interpretation
Less than 100 mg/dL Optimal Range
100 – 129 mg/dL Near Optimal / Above Optimal
130 – 159 mg/dL Borderline High
160 – 189 mg/dL High Risk Status
190 mg/dL and Above Severely Elevated Risk

Clinical Indicators & Factors

What Drives Elevated LDL?

  • Diets consistently high in saturated and trans fats.
  • Sedentary lifestyle and lack of cardiovascular exercise.
  • Genetic indicators, including Familial Hypercholesterolemia.
  • Secondary conditions, like hypothyroidism or metabolic issues.

Associated Health Risks

  • Silent Symptoms: High LDL values exhibit no immediate, everyday bodily symptoms.
  • Atherosclerosis (gradual stiffening and clogging of your arteries).
  • Elevated risk profiles for chronic angina or chest pains.
  • Heightened susceptibility to unexpected heart attacks or strokes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is pre-test fasting required for accurate results?
Historically, 9 to 12-hour fasts were mandatory. However, modern medical standards verify non-fasting baseline test panels deliver accurate evaluations for routine wellness monitoring. Defer to your processing medical team's specific guidelines.
How regularly should my lipid profile get checked?
Standard healthy adults are advised to refresh screens every 4 to 6 years. If you navigate persistent histories of diabetes, high blood pressure, or related familial cardiac indicators, schedules transition to annual tracking routines.
