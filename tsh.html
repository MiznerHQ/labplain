TSH (Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone) Test

What your TSH result actually means — explained in plain English, not medical jargon.

What Is TSH?

TSH stands for Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone. It is produced by your pituitary gland — a small gland at the base of your brain — and its job is to tell your thyroid gland how much hormone to make.

Think of TSH as the thermostat and your thyroid as the furnace. When the body needs more thyroid hormone, the pituitary turns up the TSH signal. When there's already plenty of thyroid hormone in your blood, TSH drops to tell the thyroid to slow down.

This is why the relationship feels backwards to most people: a high TSH usually means your thyroid is underperforming (hypothyroidism), while a low TSH usually means your thyroid is overperforming (hyperthyroidism).

💡 The Backwards Rule: High TSH = your pituitary is screaming at an underactive thyroid. Low TSH = your pituitary has backed off because an overactive thyroid is already making too much hormone.

TSH Reference Ranges

Most labs use a standard reference range, but ranges can shift slightly depending on the lab, your age, and whether you are pregnant.

TSH Level Range (mIU/L) Status What It Suggests
Very Low < 0.1 Critical Low Severe hyperthyroidism or thyroid storm risk
Low 0.1 – 0.39 Low Possible hyperthyroidism; often needs follow-up
Normal 0.4 – 4.0 Normal Thyroid function appears healthy
Mildly High 4.1 – 10.0 High Subclinical hypothyroidism; may or may not need treatment
Very High > 10.0 Critical High Overt hypothyroidism; treatment typically recommended
Pregnancy (1st trimester) 0.1 – 2.5 Special Range Tighter control needed; different thresholds apply

⚠️ Lab ranges vary. Always compare your result to the reference range printed on your own lab report — not internet averages. Your doctor's interpretation of your result matters more than any number alone.

Symptoms by TSH Direction

Your symptoms often tell the story before the lab result does. Here's what each direction of abnormal TSH typically feels like:

🔴 High TSH — Underactive Thyroid

  • Fatigue and sluggishness
  • Unexplained weight gain
  • Feeling cold all the time
  • Constipation
  • Dry skin and brittle nails
  • Hair thinning or loss
  • Brain fog, poor memory
  • Depression or low mood
  • Slow heart rate (bradycardia)
  • Puffy face, especially around eyes
  • Muscle aches or weakness
  • Heavy or irregular periods

🟡 Low TSH — Overactive Thyroid

  • Rapid or irregular heartbeat
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Feeling hot and sweating excessively
  • Anxiety, nervousness, irritability
  • Trembling hands or fingers
  • Diarrhea or frequent bowel movements
  • Insomnia or difficulty sleeping
  • Muscle weakness
  • Bulging eyes (Graves' disease sign)
  • Goiter (enlarged neck)
  • Light or missed periods
  • Fatigue despite feeling wired

📌 Note: Many people with mildly abnormal TSH have no symptoms at all. This is called subclinical thyroid disease and is very common, especially in older adults. Your doctor will weigh whether treatment makes sense.

What Causes Abnormal TSH?

There are many reasons your TSH can fall outside the normal range — some are serious, many are manageable.

🔴 Causes of HIGH TSH

  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune)
  • After radioactive iodine treatment
  • Post-thyroid surgery
  • Certain medications (lithium, amiodarone)
  • Iodine deficiency
  • Pituitary tumor (TSH-secreting)
  • Recovery from recent illness
  • Congenital hypothyroidism

🟡 Causes of LOW TSH

  • Graves' disease (autoimmune)
  • Toxic nodular goiter
  • Thyroiditis (temporary inflammation)
  • Too much thyroid medication
  • Pituitary gland damage or tumor
  • High-dose biotin supplements
  • Early pregnancy (normal in 1st trimester)
  • Excessive iodine intake

💊 Medication note: If you take levothyroxine (Synthroid, Tirosint), your TSH result is the primary tool your doctor uses to adjust your dose. A single off result doesn't always mean a dose change — trends over time matter more.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q My TSH is slightly high but I feel fine. Do I need treatment?
Not necessarily. Subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH 4–10 with normal T4) is often monitored rather than treated immediately, especially if you have no symptoms. Your doctor will consider your age, other risk factors, and trends over time before recommending medication.
Q Can TSH levels change from day to day?
Yes. TSH naturally fluctuates — it follows a circadian rhythm and is highest in the early morning hours. Illness, stress, poor sleep, and recent food intake can also affect the result. That's why one slightly abnormal reading often prompts a repeat test before any diagnosis is made.
Q What's the difference between TSH, T3, and T4?
TSH tells your thyroid what to do. T4 (thyroxine) is the main hormone the thyroid produces, and T3 (triiodothyronine) is the active form the body converts T4 into. TSH is usually the first test ordered because it's the most sensitive indicator of thyroid function. T3 and T4 are added when TSH is abnormal to get the full picture.
Q I'm pregnant. Are my TSH results interpreted differently?
Yes — significantly. During the first trimester, TSH naturally drops due to rising hCG levels. Most guidelines use a lower upper limit of around 2.5 mIU/L for the first trimester. Unmanaged thyroid disease during pregnancy carries real risks, so be sure your OB knows about any thyroid conditions.
Q Can I take biotin supplements before my TSH test?
No — you should stop biotin (vitamin B7) for at least 48–72 hours before a thyroid panel. High-dose biotin interferes with the lab assay and can produce falsely low TSH results, which has led to unnecessary treatment. Many people taking beauty supplements are getting biotin without realizing it.
Q Is a TSH of 3.5 considered normal?
Technically yes — 3.5 falls within the standard reference range of 0.4–4.0. However, some endocrinologists prefer TSH under 2.5 for optimal thyroid function, especially in women trying to conceive. This is an area of ongoing debate in medicine. If you feel symptomatic at 3.5, it's worth discussing with your doctor.
Q Do I need to fast before a TSH test?
Not usually. TSH results are not significantly affected by food intake, unlike glucose or cholesterol tests. However, if your test includes a broader metabolic panel, your doctor may ask you to fast anyway. When in doubt, ask your provider's office before the appointment.

Related Tests Often Ordered Together

When TSH is abnormal, your doctor will usually follow up with one or more of these tests to complete the picture:

Free T4
The main thyroid hormone; confirms whether the thyroid is actually underperforming
Free T3
The active form of thyroid hormone; helps diagnose T3 toxicosis and monitor treatment
TPO Antibodies
Detects Hashimoto's (hypothyroid) and Graves' disease (hyperthyroid)
Thyroglobulin Ab
Another autoimmune marker; often ordered alongside TPO antibodies
Glucose
Thyroid dysfunction can affect blood sugar regulation
Hemoglobin / CBC
Hypothyroidism is a common cause of unexplained anemia

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

Walk into your appointment prepared. Here are the most important questions to ask when your TSH result is abnormal:

  1. 1
    Is my TSH result high or low enough to explain my symptoms, or could something else be causing them?
  2. 2
    Should we also check my Free T4, Free T3, or thyroid antibodies to get the complete picture?
  3. 3
    Is this likely to be a temporary fluctuation, or does the trend suggest a chronic condition?
  4. 4
    Do I need treatment now, or should we retest in 3–6 months and monitor first?
  5. 5
    If I start thyroid medication, what TSH range are we aiming for?
  6. 6
    Are any of my current medications or supplements affecting my thyroid or this test result?
  7. 7
    Should I see an endocrinologist, or is this something you can manage?
  8. 8
    How often should I retest, and what symptoms should prompt me to call sooner?
